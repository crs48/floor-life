Clear a little space on the floor. From standing, lower yourself down to sitting, then stand back up. Try to use no hands, no knees, nothing to lean on. Start with a mental ten and subtract a point for each support you use on the way down and on the way up.

That's the Sitting–Rising Test, and it has spent the last decade quietly becoming one of the most talked-about numbers in longevity.

## Where the headline comes from

In 2012, a team in Rio de Janeiro published a study of just over two thousand adults aged 51 to 80. They scored each person's sit-and-rise, then followed them for about six years. The pattern was hard to ignore: **each additional point was associated with roughly 21% better survival**, and the people who scored lowest died at five to six times the rate of those who scored highest.

A larger follow-up in 2025 — more than four thousand people, tracked for a median of twelve years — found the same shape, only sharper. A perfect ten came with about a 3.7% death rate over the period; the lowest scores, around 42%.

Those are big numbers. It's easy to see why the test went viral.

## How to read it honestly

Here's the part the viral version leaves out. The test doesn't measure a habit. It measures **musculoskeletal fitness** — a blend of strength, flexibility, balance, and body composition. People who can float up off the floor tend to be fit, and fit people tend to live longer. The score is a *marker*, not a *mechanism*.

So no, you can't practise the test into a longer life the way you'd take a pill. What you *can* do is train the underlying capacity — the hip and ankle range, the leg strength, the balance — and floor living happens to be a pleasant, low-effort way to keep all of it. Every time a room without chairs makes you get down and back up, you're rehearsing exactly the thing the test rewards.

> A good score is a starting line, not a fortune. A poor score is a to-do list, not a sentence.

## What to actually do with your number

- If you scored **eight or higher**, protect it. A few minutes of floor time a day keeps the range you already have.
- If you landed in the **middle**, that's the sweet spot for improvement — props, short sits, and easy squats move the needle fastest here.
- If you **struggled**, build up gently, ideally with a professional. Getting up more easily is a trainable skill, not a fixed trait.

And then mostly forget the score. The point was never the test. It was the getting up and down.
