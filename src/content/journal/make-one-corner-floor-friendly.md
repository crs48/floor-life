Most people who try floor living quit for the same reason: they treat it as a renovation. They picture a bare, furniture-free apartment and, quite reasonably, decide that's not their life. So they do nothing.

There's a much smaller first step, and it works far better.

## Choose one corner

Not a room. A corner. Somewhere you already like to be — beside a window, next to the bookshelf, wherever the afternoon light lands. You're going to make that one spot the single most inviting place in the room to be low to the ground.

Here's the whole recipe:

- **A firm rug** over a dense pad, so the surface is comfortable but stable.
- **One good cushion** — a buckwheat zafu is ideal — to raise your hips above your knees.
- **A low table** within arm's reach, for a cup of tea, a book, a laptop.
- **A plant** and **warm light**. This is the part people skip, and it's the part that makes you want to stay.

That's it. You haven't thrown anything away. You haven't committed to anything. You've just built a small, beautiful trap.

## Let the corner do the work

The mistake is thinking floor living takes willpower. It doesn't, if you set it up right. When the floor is genuinely the nicest place in the room — softer light, better view, a warm surface, your book already there — you'll drift down to it without deciding to. The environment does the deciding.

> Don't try to sit on the floor more. Make one place where sitting on the floor is the obvious thing to do.

Give it a week. You'll notice you've started reading down there, or taking calls, or just landing there at the end of the day. When the corner is full more often than not, grow it: a second cushion, a lower chair nearby, a spot for a guest.

Floor living isn't a vow. It's a corner that keeps getting bigger.
